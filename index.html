Project idea

Outline

The IT project, Double Check, is a web extension designed to screen and simplify a website’s terms and conditions and can be applied when the user wants to check without scanning and reading each clause. The extension will compile each clause into bullet points that, when toggle, can describe to the user what they mean in layman’s terms with an example. The bullets can then be sent to an email as a document for easier reading and review. Overall, the web extension will be designed to provide more security and comfort to the users in handling a tedious task in a simple manner.

Motivation

In such a digitised world, we heavily rely on platforms to carry out our daily needs and wants; yet there are still huge risks to companies and customers. Privacy and data protection are increasingly becoming difficult mediums to regulate and utilise without avoiding possibilities of failure, being compromised or even extortion. According to an article published in 2018 on the Conversation that 94% of Australians do not read all privacy policies that apply to them. Yet we do not have the time to even read through half of them as it would take 244 hours for the average person to work through each term and condition that would apply to them and even more to check when a website has changed theirs. The Consumer Policy Research Centre’s report stated that while 91% of Australians are okay with services using their personal data for business reasons, 95% of Australians would also like options to avoid data collection. It is not surprising with the number of people but rather how many become affected or fall victim to it. And in the wake of the recent scandal involving breaches in privacy with Facebook in 2019 with 533 million peoples’ data published on a hacking forum; users should be more attentive and knowledgeable about how their data is being used by companies and other platforms. Thus, Double-Check would provide support and comfort in protecting users’ data and privacy with a simple reading task in an easier process.

Breakdown

Double Check would be a downloadable web extension that allows users to summarise any term and conditions document. Once turned on and met with its first task, it would scan through clauses, looking for common phrases and target essential details such as access and usage to users’ data or privacy. Once completed, the document will be summed up into bullet points that the user can click on to view what that particular clause would intend for the user. It could provide a possible example or even highlight obvious potential risks that might be considered to be vital to them. Such valuables could be phone contacts, messages, device ID, location, social networks, or browsing history. The user can double click to be taken to the specific clause in question so that the user can screenshot it or even read it thoroughly. If the web extension cannot recognise the text as the terms and conditions, the user can instead copy it to an online document like Google Docs so the extension can scan it. This will be utilised by checking off a box to enable the web extension to access and scan such online documents. Finally, the web extension will ask the user whether they would like a word document with all the bullet points and summaries sent to their email so they can print, store or review the scanned terms and conditions. Add-ons could include bringing up first specified topics or essential details such as user access and personal information usage to allow users to pick and choose relevant concerns that apply to them. Another could also include screenshots of those clauses alongside the document with the bullet points to be sent to the users’ emails.

Tools

The tools required to build Double Check would most likely need various coding software such as HTML or Javascript. But the leading software would have to be able to scan, read, interpret and provide feedback to users to ensure that they can comprehend what terms and conditions are being presented to them. A coding program called common lisp, a dialect of the Lisp programming language, could be utilised to read the text and interpret the meaning for the user. Lisp is known as list processing and is used to manipulate data strings; commonly used for artificial intelligence programming. Common Lisp is used in the famous web extension, app and browser: Grammarly, which scans, reads and helps users with texts that they write. Its strengths are processing speed and compilation rate, which would have to be considered to keep up with ever-changing terms and conditions.
